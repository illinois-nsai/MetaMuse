Slide 1 — Title (0:00–0:35)
“Hi everyone, I’m Ruiying Ma, presenting MetaMuse: Algorithm Generation via Creative Ideation. This work asks a simple question: can LLMs actually generate new system algorithms, not just rephrase known heuristics? We study this in two production‑critical tasks—cache replacement and online bin pac
king—and show large improvements: up to 35.76% fewer cache misses and 30.93% fewer bins used. We also see markedly higher diversity in generated solu
tions. I’ll first show the core challenge, then the MetaMuse framework, and finally the experimental results.” (arxiv.org
(https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))

Slide 2 — Challenge: LLM Availability Bias (0:35–1:15)
“Repeatedly sampling strong LLMs doesn’t yield genuinely new algorithms. Instead, solutions cluster around familiar heuristics—LRU, LFU, FIFO for cac
hing, and similarly standard policies in other domains. This is availability bias: the model keeps producing high‑probability ideas from its training
distribution. We see the same clustering across GPT‑4o, Llama‑3.3‑70B, and DeepSeek‑V3, so it’s not a single model quirk—it’s a structural tendency of
next‑token prediction. Turning up temperature only weakly mitigates the bias and quickly degrades coherence.” (arxiv.org
(https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))

Slide 3 — MetaMuse Overview: Three Self‑Reflection Principles (1:15–2:00)
“MetaMuse reframes algorithm generation as creative ideation in a discontinuous solution space. It uses three self‑reflection principles.
First, performance‑based diversity evaluation: we measure diversity in the feedback space—actual performance outcomes—not just textual differences.
Second, external stimuli steering: instead of hoping randomness yields novelty, we inject external stimuli—like keywords—so the model associates with
knowledge that seems unrelated to the task.
Third, waypoint reasoning: rather than free‑form chain‑of‑thought, we break the reasoning into structured checkpoints to turn stimuli into executable
algorithms.” (arxiv.org (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))

Slide 4 — RSDict‑SF: Learning to Steer Better (2:00–2:40)
“To pick good stimuli, MetaMuse uses two strategies: a random dictionary sampler, and the stronger RSDict‑SF. RSDict‑SF learns from prior iterations.
It represents each solution by a feedback embedding that captures diversity and usefulness, then sets a steering direction for either exploration or
exploitation. It predicts which candidate stimulus set will likely move toward that target using Gaussian Process Regression. This lets MetaMuse adap
tively balance “try something new” versus “push performance.”” (arxiv.org (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))

Slide 5 — Application 1: Cache Replacement (2:40–3:25)
“We evaluate on 96 real‑world cache traces, including RetrievalAttention traces and production storage traces from Tencent and Alibaba. Cache capacity
is 10% of distinct objects. MetaMuse is compared against repeated sampling, ReEvo, MCTS‑AHD, OpenEvolve, PlanSearch, and human heuristics. Across GPT
‑4o, Llama‑3.3‑70B, and DeepSeek‑V3, MetaMuse achieves the best miss‑ratio reductions—up to 35.76% fewer misses versus FIFO, and strong gains over ot
her LLM‑based methods.” (arxiv.org (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))

Slide 6 — Application 2: Online Bin Packing (3:25–4:05)
“In bin packing we use 288 traces: Falkenauer‑U and Scholl‑1 from BPPLIB, plus Weibull and Gaussian‑generated workloads. Baselines are the same. Meta
Muse reduces bin usage by up to 30.93% versus First Fit, and again outperforms other LLM‑driven approaches. This shows the framework generalizes acro
ss different systems problems, not just caching.” (arxiv.org (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))

Slide 7 — Diversity & Entropy (4:05–4:35)
“Beyond raw performance, we explicitly measure diversity. MetaMuse yields about 1.8× more distinct solutions and about 1.3× higher entropy in the sol
ution distribution compared to the best baselines. Waypoint reasoning is a key driver here: removing it significantly drops the number of distinct so
lutions across all three LLM backbones.” (arxiv.org (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))

Slide 8 — Summary (4:35–5:00)
“To wrap up: MetaMuse tackles availability bias with three self‑reflection principles, steers ideation with learned stimuli selection, and produces a
lgorithms that are both better and more diverse. In production‑scale workloads, it delivers up to 35.76% cache‑miss reduction and 30.93% bin‑usage re
duction, while expanding the solution space rather than collapsing to known heuristics. Thanks—happy to take questions.” (arxiv.org
(https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.03851))
